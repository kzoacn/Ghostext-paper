\section{Introduction}

Steganography and cryptography solve different problems. Cryptography protects the content of a message; steganography hides the fact that communication is happening at all. On a modern text channel, the ideal cover is text that an observer would attribute to ordinary use---often, ordinary output of a large language model.

\input{figures/intro-steganography}

Figure~\ref{fig:steganography-channel} gives the intuition. Plaintext and obvious ciphertext invite blocking, while ordinary-looking text can pass through a monitored channel without revealing that a hidden exchange is taking place.

Suppose Alice and Bob each run the same LLM and share a passphrase, and Alice wants to send a secret message to Bob over a channel watched by a passive censor. A natural attempt is to turn the plaintext into bits and steer the LLM's token choices with those bits, as in Neural Linguistic Steganography~\cite{ziegler2019neural} and subsequent generation-based work~\cite{huang2026odstega,zhang2021provably,ding2023discop,dewitt2024perfect}. Two systems-level questions remain. What exact bitstream is handed to the cover generator, and can it be made to look indistinguishable from random? How does Bob reliably replay Alice's process when tokenizer boundaries, truncation policies, and backend configuration all have to agree?

Ghostext answers both with a two-layer pipeline. A cryptographic packet layer wraps the plaintext into an authenticated packet whose bytes are pseudorandom under the passphrase. A token-selection layer then embeds that packet into cover text via deterministic candidate construction and arithmetic-coding-style interval narrowing, using the same truncation policy the model would use for natural sampling on the same prompt. Decoding is deterministic only when Alice and Bob share prompt and decoding policy; any drift in this shared state---or any modification in transit---causes decoding to fail before plaintext is released. We position the design as \emph{near-distribution-preserving}: against the same-policy natural-cover baseline, Ghostext's per-step distribution deviates only through deterministic quantization and a retokenization-stability filter. We do not claim perfect security in the stronger information-theoretic sense of Cachin, Hopper et al., or de~Witt et al.~\cite{cachin1998information,hopper2002provably,dewitt2024perfect}.

\paragraph{Contributions.} (i) A protocol that combines authenticated packet framing and retokenization-aware candidate control in a single deterministic pipeline. (ii) An end-to-end implementation with sender and receiver sharing prompt and decoding policy. (iii) A measured evaluation on an open-source LLM backend, using a fixed $10 \times 10$ prompt-message grid to report decode reliability, latency, and payload density under a pinned local configuration.

\paragraph{Scope.} Evidence is implementation-level: decode recoverability and overhead under a pinned runtime. Detector-facing resistance, robustness to active text modification, and cross-hardware determinism are out of scope.
